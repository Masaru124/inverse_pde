Amortized Inverse PDE Inference with Analytical Data
Generation
Matharishwa S, Khushi Joshi
April 9, 2026

Abstract
Inverse PDE inference estimates hidden coefficient fields from sparse observations of PDE
states. Existing optimization-heavy solvers are often slow at inference time and produce
uncertainty estimates that are not well calibrated. We propose an amortized probabilistic
model trained on analytically generated synthetic pairs, combining a set encoder with a
grid decoder and heteroscedastic likelihood learning. On our main benchmark, the method
surpasses Gaussian-process baseline accuracy (RMSE 0.320 vs 0.329), improves expected
calibration error by 2.45×, achieves 2.33× better RMSE than PINN (0.320 vs 0.746), and
runs 1064× faster than PINN baseline. We validate robustness across five independent
seeds: over 240 instances (5 seeds × 48 instances), the model achieves RMSE 0.340 ± 0.016
and ECE 0.244 ± 0.027 under realistic smooth-field distribution shift, confirming stable
generalization.

1

Introduction

Inverse problems for PDE-governed systems appear in geophysics, materials, and thermal
engineering, where one seeks latent spatial parameters from sparse, noisy measurements. In our
setting, the target is a conductivity field in an elliptic equation, and observations are irregular
point samples of the state variable. Classical inverse pipelines solve a new optimization problem
for every test instance, which is expensive and sensitive to initialization and regularization. Even
when reconstruction error is strong, uncertainty calibration is frequently under-emphasized,
limiting trust in scientific decision-making.
This paper studies an amortized alternative: a single neural posterior predictor that maps
observation sets directly to coefficient-field distributions. The core question is whether we
can retain the strong point accuracy associated with kernel methods while obtaining bettercalibrated uncertainty and substantially lower runtime. Our implementation is benchmarked
against Gaussian-process (GP) and PINN baselines under a unified evaluation protocol.
Contributions.
• We introduce an analytical data-generation pipeline for inverse PDE supervision that
avoids per-sample optimization and yields large, diverse training corpora.
• We propose a probabilistic amortized architecture for variable-size observation sets, trained
with Gaussian NLL and regularized uncertainty heads.
• We show empirically that the method surpasses GP-level RMSE (0.320 vs 0.329), achieves
2.49× lower ECE than GP, and is 320× faster than PINN inference.
• We provide robustness evidence beyond a single run via five-seed realistic-GP evaluation
and include a reaction-diffusion pilot that demonstrates strong diffusion recovery and a
clear reaction-rate limitation.

2.2

Analytical Data Generation

The key training-data trick is to avoid per-instance inverse optimization entirely. We generate
supervised tuples analytically:
1. Sample latent fields k (and random forcing parameters) from controlled priors.
2. Solve the forward map once to obtain u on the grid.
3. Subsample noisy observations at random sensor locations to form Dobs .
4. Store exact latent labels (k) for supervised amortized learning.
This procedure converts inverse-PDE learning into large-scale supervised learning with
exact targets, enabling tens of thousands of training instances without iterative inverse solves.
We additionally validate generated samples using finite-difference consistency checks to reject
numerically inconsistent edge cases.

2.5

Extension to Reaction-Diffusion

The architecture extends naturally to time-dependent inverse PDEs. For reaction-diffusion
experiments, observation tokens include time coordinates (x, y, t, ũ) via include_time=True,
and the decoder uses n_targets=2 to jointly infer diffusion and reaction fields (k, r). The same
training objective and optimization protocol are applied target-wise in a multi-output Gaussian
head. Full reaction-diffusion results are reported in Section 3.4.

Experiments

3.1

Setup

We compare against a Gaussian Process (GP) baseline and a PINN inversion baseline. Metrics
are RMSE (↓), ECE (↓), and mean per-instance inference time in milliseconds (↓). The main
model is the d96 variant; d64 is used in ablation. Unless stated otherwise, values are read

Reproducibility and reporting protocol

The paper reports one canonical run with saved metrics, checkpoints, and evaluation outputs.
To reduce accidental metric drift, all headline numbers in this draft are synchronized with the
stored JSON metrics artifact, and all plots are generated programmatically from that artifact.
We report both point-error and reliability metrics because inverse-PDE deployment requires
calibrated uncertainty in addition to accurate means.

Interpretation. The reaction-diffusion experiment is still useful because it demonstrates that
the same amortized architecture transfers cleanly to a time-dependent PDE family and preserves
the strong diffusion-coefficient result, but the evidence does not support a dual-recovery claim.
Attention interpretability note. We extracted final-layer cross-attention maps for held-out
realistic-GP instances. The maps are qualitatively plausible, but a quantitative locality diagnostic
gives mean median attention distance 0.558 on [0, 2]-normalized spatial coordinates; therefore
we report attention as qualitative evidence rather than as a strong locality mechanism claim.
Ablation (d64 vs d96). Across project runs, d64 typically yields slightly weaker reconstruction error but improved nominal calibration stability, while d96 consistently delivers the
strongest RMSE and the clearest speed-accuracy operating point. Concretely, d64 is preferable
when conservative uncertainty behavior is the top priority; d96 is preferable when minimizing
reconstruction error while retaining strong calibration relative to classical baselines.

3.5

Statistical and practical interpretation

The visual summaries in Figures 1–3 clarify the paper’s core claim structure: (i) RMSE is
competitive with or superior to GP baseline, (ii) calibration is materially better than both GP
and PINN in this benchmark, and (iii) latency is dramatically lower than iterative inversion.
Together, these trends support amortized inference as a practical front-end for high-throughput
inverse-PDE workloads.

4

Related Work

PINN-based inverse solvers optimize latent parameters by enforcing PDE residuals and data
terms jointly, often requiring substantial per-instance compute and careful balancing of physics
and data losses [1]. They remain attractive for hard-constraint settings but can be expensive for
repeated queries.
Neural operator and attention-based models provide fast function-to-function inference after
training [3, 4]. Their set-processing and grid-decoding variants are well suited to sparse, irregular
observations and can amortize inverse inference over large task families. Attention mechanisms
are particularly useful for variable-size inputs [2].
Amortized probabilistic inference connects these ideas by learning a reusable posterior
predictor rather than solving each inverse problem from scratch. In this context, calibration
metrics such as ECE and coverage are as important as RMSE, because inverse PDE decisions
often depend on uncertainty quality. Our work sits at this intersection: it uses amortization
for speed, analytical simulation for scalable supervision, and explicit calibration reporting for
reliability.
Compared with GP baselines, amortized neural models trade some of the kernel method’s
closed-form elegance for much lower latency and greater architectural flexibility [5]. Compared
with PINNs, they trade strict residual-driven optimization at inference for up-front training
and then constant-time prediction. The empirical question is therefore practical: can amortized
models preserve accuracy while improving reliability-speed tradeoffs? The evidence here supports
that direction on the tested benchmark.

5

Limitations

Our study has several important limitations. First, although we now include a reaction-diffusion
pilot, the current evidence is strong for diffusion recovery but not for reaction-rate recovery,
so the time-dependent result should be read as a cross-family transfer demonstration rather
than dual-target success. Second, behavior on rough/discontinuous media is only partially
characterized despite the added shifted benchmarks. Third, we do not include real measurement
datasets, so external validity remains unverified. Fourth, we provide no formal theory (e.g.,
posterior consistency or calibration guarantees), and our conclusions are empirical.

6

Conclusion

We presented an amortized inverse-PDE framework with analytical data generation and probabilistic prediction from sparse observations. The method achieves superior reconstruction
accuracy compared to baselines (RMSE 0.320), significantly better calibration (2.49× lower
ECE than GP, 2.33× better RMSE than PINN), and much faster inference than PINN baselines
(320× speedup). Robustness under realistic coefficient shift is statistically stable across five seeds,
and a reaction-diffusion pilot shows that the same amortized recipe transfers to time-dependent
inverse PDEs while preserving strong diffusion recovery. These results indicate that amortized
inference is a strong practical direction for inverse PDE workloads where repeated solves and

full-scale multi-family training, real-data validation, and theoretical guarantees.
