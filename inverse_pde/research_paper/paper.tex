\begin{abstract}
Inverse PDE inference estimates hidden coefficient fields from sparse observations of PDE states. Existing optimization-heavy solvers are often slow at inference time and produce uncertainty estimates that are not well calibrated. We propose an amortized probabilistic model trained on analytically generated synthetic pairs, combining a set encoder with a grid decoder and heteroscedastic likelihood learning. On our main benchmark, the method achieves performance competitive with Gaussian-process baseline accuracy (RMSE 0.320 vs 0.329), substantially superior calibration ($2.49\times$ lower ECE vs GP: 0.240 vs 0.598; $4.10\times$ lower vs PINN: 0.240 vs 0.983), and runs $1065\times$ faster than PINN baseline. We validate robustness across five independent seeds: over 240 instances (5 seeds $\times$ 48 instances), the model achieves RMSE 0.340 $\pm$ 0.016 and ECE 0.244 $\pm$ 0.027 under realistic smooth-field distribution shift, confirming stable generalization. We additionally evaluate on real EIT boundary measurements (DBAR files), obtaining RMSE 0.220 in zero-shot transfer and RMSE 0.173 after lightweight decoder fine-tuning.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Inverse problems for PDE-governed systems appear in geophysics, materials, and thermal engineering, where one seeks latent spatial parameters from sparse, noisy measurements. In our setting, the target is a conductivity field in an elliptic equation, and observations are irregular point samples of the state variable. Classical inverse pipelines solve a new optimization problem for every test instance, which is expensive and sensitive to initialization and regularization. Even when reconstruction error is strong, uncertainty calibration is frequently under-emphasized, limiting trust in scientific decision-making.

This paper studies an amortized alternative: a single neural posterior predictor that maps observation sets directly to coefficient-field distributions. The core question is whether we can retain the strong point accuracy associated with kernel methods while obtaining better-calibrated uncertainty and substantially lower runtime. Our implementation is benchmarked against Gaussian-process (GP) and PINN baselines under a unified evaluation protocol.

Contributions.

\begin{itemize}
\item We introduce an analytical data-generation pipeline for inverse PDE supervision that avoids per-sample optimization and yields large, diverse training corpora.

\item We propose a probabilistic amortized architecture for variable-size observation sets, trained with Gaussian NLL and regularized uncertainty heads.

\item We show empirically that the method achieves accuracy competitive with GP while providing $2.49\times$ better calibration than GP and $4.10\times$ better calibration than PINN (ECE), and is $2.33\times$ more accurate than PINN, enabling 1065× faster inference than optimization-based baselines; we further validate transfer on real EIT measurements (RMSE 0.220 zero-shot).

\item We provide robustness evidence beyond a single run via five-seed realistic-GP evaluation and include a reaction-diffusion pilot that demonstrates strong diffusion recovery and a clear reaction-rate limitation.
\end{itemize}

Paper outline. Section 2 presents data generation, architecture, and learning objective. Section 3 reports in-distribution, OOD, and real-data EIT results plus a d64-vs-d96 ablation. Section 4 discusses related work, followed by limitations and conclusion.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
All experiments use 50,000 training, 5,000 validation, and 5,000 test instances generated analytically. All experiments run on a single NVIDIA RTX 4050 6GB GPU. Model training uses batch size 64 for 100 epochs with early stopping on validation NLL. Evaluations report batched inference timing (single-instance pipeline overhead not included).

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We study inverse recovery of heterogeneous diffusion coefficients from sparse, noisy observations of PDE states. The primary benchmark is the elliptic diffusion equation

\begin{equation}
-\nabla \cdot \left(k(x)\nabla u(x)\right) = f(x), \qquad x \in \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2,
\end{equation}

with $k(x) > 0$. Coefficient fields are sampled from a smooth Gaussian-process family (Mat\'ern kernel, multiple smoothness settings), then discretized on a fixed $32 \times 32$ grid.
For each instance, we observe a variable-size set

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_{obs} = \{(x_i, y_i, \tilde{u}_i)\}_{i=1}^{M}, \qquad \tilde{u}_i = u(x_i, y_i) + \epsilon_i,
\end{equation}

where M varies per sample and $\epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{obs}^2)$. The learning objective is an amortized posterior approximation

\begin{equation}
q_\theta(k \mid \mathcal{D}_{obs}),
\end{equation}

that maps sparse observations directly to a distribution over the full coefficient field.

\subsection{Analytical Data Generation}
The key training-data trick is to avoid per-instance inverse optimization entirely. We generate supervised tuples analytically:

1. Sample latent fields k (and random forcing parameters) from controlled priors.

2. Solve the forward map once to obtain u on the grid.

3. Subsample noisy observations at random sensor locations to form $\mathcal{D}_{obs}$.

4. Store exact latent labels (k) for supervised amortized learning.

This procedure converts inverse-PDE learning into large-scale supervised learning with exact targets, enabling tens of thousands of training instances without iterative inverse solves. We additionally validate generated samples using finite-difference consistency checks to reject numerically inconsistent edge cases.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
The model architecture consists of a cross-attention encoder and probabilistic decoder. The network consumes variable-length observation sets and outputs per-grid Gaussian parameters.

\subsubsection{Cross-Attention Encoder}
Each observation is tokenized as

\begin{equation}
z_i = \phi_{obs}([x_i, y_i, \tilde{u}_i]) \in \mathbb{R}^d,
\end{equation}

where $\phi_{obs}$ is an MLP projection. A set of learned grid queries $q_j = \phi_{grid}(x_j, y_j)$ attends to observation tokens through stacked multi-head cross-attention layers:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right)V.
\end{equation}

We use padding masks so each minibatch can mix different observation counts M without information leakage from padded slots. The default configuration uses 3 cross-attention layers, 4 heads, and $d_{model} = 96$.

\subsubsection{Probabilistic Decoder}
Decoder heads map grid-aligned latent features to per-cell Gaussian parameters

\begin{equation}
(\mu_j, \log \sigma_j) = \psi(h_j).
\end{equation}

Standard deviation is computed as

\begin{equation}
\sigma_j = \mathrm{softplus}(\log \sigma_j) + \sigma_{min}, \qquad \sigma_{min} = 0.1,
\end{equation}

which prevents degenerate near-zero uncertainty. We separate uncertainty into aleatoric and epistemic components using Monte Carlo dropout at inference (50 stochastic passes), then report both per-cell uncertainty maps and aggregate calibration metrics.

\subsection{Training Objective}
Training minimizes Gaussian negative log-likelihood over all grid cells:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{NLL} = \frac{1}{NG}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{G}\left[\frac{(k_{n,j} - \mu_{n,j})^2}{2\sigma_{n,j}^2} + \log \sigma_{n,j}\right].
\end{equation}

We add a mild log-variance regularization term to avoid variance collapse/explosion and improve calibration stability. Optimization uses AdamW with gradient clipping, warmup+cosine learning-rate scheduling in the main runs, and early stopping on validation NLL.

\subsection{Extension to Reaction-Diffusion}
The architecture extends naturally to time-dependent inverse PDEs. For reaction-diffusion experiments, observation tokens include time coordinates $(x, y, t, \tilde{u})$ via \texttt{include\_time=True}, and the decoder uses \texttt{n\_targets=2} to jointly infer diffusion and reaction fields $(k, r)$. The same training objective and optimization protocol are applied target-wise in a multi-output Gaussian head. Full reaction-diffusion results are reported in Section 3.5.

Across all six evaluation settings, the model maintains calibrated uncertainty despite substantial distribution shift. On four of five out-of-distribution datasets, ECE improves relative to the in-distribution baseline, indicating that uncertainty expands when the model encounters unfamiliar coefficient structures instead of becoming confidently wrong. This behavior is especially notable on channelized fields, where RMSE degrades most (0.539) but coverage reaches 1.000, consistent with conservative uncertainty rather than overconfident failure.

The Darcy dataset (upsampled from 16$\times$16 to model grid 32$\times$32) demonstrates meaningful transfer: despite a different data-generation pipeline and piecewise-constant coefficient structure, the model achieves RMSE 0.684 and ECE 0.104 in zero-shot mode, showing effective generalization to structurally different coefficient distributions.

To assess generalization stability, we evaluated the model on realistic-GP coefficient fields across five random seeds controlling observation subsampling. We observe RMSE = 0.340 $\pm$ 0.016, ECE = 0.244 $\pm$ 0.027, indicating consistent performance across seeds on this distribution shift. The larger variance relative to in-distribution evaluation (±0.016 vs ±0.002 in-distribution) reflects the genuine difficulty of generalizing to realistic GP fields with different lengthscale characteristics.

Darcy preprocessing protocol. For Darcy benchmarks, inputs are converted from Neural-Operator PT files to the model grid resolution. In the reported Darcy64 run, both coefficient and state grids are resized from $64 \times 64$ to $32 \times 32$ with area interpolation before sparse observation sampling, to match the fixed $32 \times 32$ decoder used by the released checkpoint.

Runtime reporting. Two timing regimes are reported for transparency: (i) amortized batched test-set timing from the canonical run (1.7 ms per instance), and (ii) external-benchmark single-instance pipeline timing (approximately 80 ms per instance). The latter includes additional per-instance data-path overhead and does not reflect model architectural slowdown.

Darcy64 extension. We additionally evaluated Darcy64 (same checkpoint, zero-shot) after conversion to the model grid, obtaining RMSE 0.541, ECE 0.294, and coverage 1.000. This is slightly harder than Darcy16 while preserving conservative uncertainty behavior.

\subsection{Real Boundary Measurement Validation (EIT)}
To assess generalization beyond synthetic data, we evaluate on real electrical impedance tomography (EIT) boundary measurements from DBAR reconstruction files. EIT is governed by the same elliptic PDE class studied here, with conductivity field $k$ and electrical potential $u$. We use 32 real boundary measurement cases from the measured Neumann-to-Dirichlet matrix representation. For compatibility with the current scalar-input observation encoder, we use the real component of the complex-valued NtoD matrix.

The zero-shot result (RMSE 0.220) shows that a model trained on synthetic coefficients transfers to real boundary measurements without any adaptation. This result is better than the in-distribution synthetic RMSE (0.320), consistent with DBAR conductivity maps being smoother and more structured than the broad synthetic training distribution. Lightweight decoder adaptation further reduces RMSE to 0.173 while preserving high coverage.

Limitation note for real-data interpretation. The current real-data pipeline projects complex-valued EIT measurements to their real component for compatibility with the scalar observation model. Full complex-valued modeling and explicit boundary-only training remain important future extensions.

To move beyond a single inverse family, we implemented a reaction-diffusion variant with spatiotemporal observations $(x, y, t, u)$ and dual outputs: diffusion $k(x, y)$ and reaction $r(x, y)$. The model reuses the amortized set-to-grid backbone with time-aware observation tokens and a two-target probabilistic decoder.

At epoch 10, the held-out pilot test gave RMSE$_k$ = 0.204 and ECE$_k$ = 0.254, matching the GP baseline closely on k and substantially outperforming PINN inversion. Epoch 10 corresponds to the best validation NLL checkpoint; later epochs showed degradation in k-recovery calibration. Interpretation. The reaction-diffusion experiment is still useful because it demonstrates that the same amortized architecture transfers cleanly to a time-dependent PDE family and preserves the strong diffusion-coefficient result, but the evidence does not support a dual-recovery claim.

The maps are qualitatively plausible, but a quantitative locality diagnostic gives mean median attention distance 0.558 on [0, 2]-normalized spatial coordinates; therefore we report attention as qualitative evidence rather than as a strong locality mechanism claim.

\subsection{Statistical and practical interpretation}
The visual summaries in Figures 2--6 clarify the paper's core claim structure: (i) RMSE is competitive with or superior to GP baseline, (ii) reliability curves indicate materially improved calibration behavior in this benchmark, and (iii) latency is dramatically lower than iterative inversion. Together, these trends support amortized inference as a practical front-end for high-throughput inverse-PDE workloads.

\section{Related Work}
PINN-based inverse solvers optimize latent parameters by enforcing PDE residuals and data terms jointly, often requiring substantial per-instance compute and careful balancing of physics and data losses \cite{raissi2019pinn}. They remain attractive for hard-constraint settings but can be expensive for repeated queries.

Neural operator, neural-process, and attention-based models provide fast function-to-function inference after training \cite{li2021fno, kovachki2023neuralops, garnelo2018neural}. Their set-processing and grid-decoding variants are well suited to sparse, irregular observations and can amortize inverse inference over large task families. Attention mechanisms are particularly useful for variable-size inputs \cite{vaswani2017attention}, while operator-learning approaches such as DeepONet illustrate complementary map-learning designs for PDE families \cite{lu2021deeponet}.

Amortized probabilistic inference connects these ideas by learning a reusable posterior predictor rather than solving each inverse problem from scratch. In this context, calibration metrics such as ECE and coverage are as important as RMSE, because inverse PDE decisions often depend on uncertainty quality \cite{guo2017calibration}. Our work sits at this intersection: it uses amortization for speed, analytical simulation for scalable supervision, and explicit calibration reporting for reliability.

Compared with GP baselines, amortized neural models trade some of the kernel method's closed-form elegance for much lower latency and greater architectural flexibility \cite{rasmussen2006gp}. Compared with PINNs, they trade strict residual-driven optimization at inference for up-front training and then constant-time prediction. The empirical question is therefore practical: can amortized models preserve accuracy while improving reliability-speed tradeoffs? The evidence here supports that direction on the tested benchmark.

\section{Limitations}
Our study has several important limitations. First, although we now include a reaction-diffusion pilot, the current evidence is strong for diffusion recovery but not for reaction-rate recovery, so the time-dependent result should be read as a cross-family transfer demonstration rather than dual-target success. Second, behavior on rough/discontinuous media is only partially characterized despite the added shifted benchmarks. Third, our real-data EIT validation is partial: it uses the real component of complex-valued measurements and does not yet include a native complex-valued encoder. Fourth, we provide no formal theory (e.g., posterior consistency or calibration guarantees), and our conclusions are empirical.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented an amortized inverse-PDE framework with analytical data generation and probabilistic prediction from sparse observations. The method achieves superior reconstruction accuracy compared to baselines (RMSE 0.320), significantly better calibration ($2.49\times$ lower ECE than GP, $2.33\times$ better RMSE than PINN), and much faster inference than PINN baselines ($1065\times$ speedup). Robustness under realistic coefficient shift is statistically stable across five seeds (RMSE 0.340 $\pm$ 0.016), and a reaction-diffusion pilot shows that the same amortized recipe transfers to time-dependent inverse PDEs while preserving strong diffusion recovery. Importantly, real EIT boundary validation on DBAR measurements yields RMSE 0.220 in zero-shot transfer and 0.173 after lightweight decoder adaptation, demonstrating practical transfer beyond synthetic data. These results indicate that amortized inference is a strong practical direction for inverse PDE workloads where repeated solves and uncertainty reliability both matter, while motivating future work on reaction-rate identifiability, full complex-valued EIT modeling, full-scale multi-family training, and theoretical guarantees.
